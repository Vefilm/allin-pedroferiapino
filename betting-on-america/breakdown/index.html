01, The Crime

It is not insider trading. Not technically. That is the entire problem.

The legal architecture around insider trading was built for stock markets. It assumes a fiduciary relationship — you have a duty to a company, and you exploit information that violates that duty. Prediction markets do not have companies. They have outcomes. Nobody owes a duty to an outcome.

This means an Army sergeant who places a Polymarket bet on a military strike he is participating in — using advance knowledge no market participant could access — may not technically be committing the crime the word "insider trading" describes. He is charged with wire fraud instead. The charge fits, barely, but it is a workaround. The underlying problem is structural: the law has not caught up with the product.

"What we are documenting is not a flaw in Polymarket. It is a feature. The platform operates in a space where the ordinary rules of financial markets were never written to apply."

David Kovel, retired CFTC attorney, largest CFTC whistleblower award recipient in history

What Polymarket actually is

Polymarket is a prediction market. Users buy shares in outcomes: "Will the US strike Iran before October?" "Will there be a ceasefire in Gaza by December?" "Will the Fed cut rates?" The price of a share reflects the market's implied probability of that outcome. If you buy a "Yes" share at 14 cents and the event happens, you receive $1. If it does not, your share is worth zero.

This is, in the CFTC's own language, an "event contract." Congress tried to create one in 2003 — the DARPA Policy Analysis Market, designed to aggregate intelligence on geopolitical events. Congress shut it down in 72 hours. The objection: it was unseemly to bet on wars and assassinations, and it created obvious incentives to cause the events being wagered on.

In 2024, the CFTC approved the identical concept. The same product. A different administration. A different outcome.

Why no government ID matters

Every regulated US financial platform requires Know Your Customer verification. Stock brokers verify identity. Even sports betting apps require identity confirmation. Polymarket, operating offshore on the Polygon blockchain, requires nothing. A username. An email. An Ethereum wallet. That is the complete onboarding process.

This means the accounts that produced a 98% win rate on US military strikes — analyzed by ACDC and Bubblemaps in April 2026 — cannot be connected to individuals without a subpoena and blockchain forensics. The CFTC has not opened an investigation. No subpoena has been sought.

The statistical case

The baseline win rate for military longshots on Polymarket — bets placed at odds of 14% or lower — is consistent with the stated probability. If a market is priced at 14%, you expect it to resolve "Yes" approximately 14% of the time across a large sample. That is how a functioning prediction market works.

The nine wallets hit at 98% on these same markets. Across a sufficient sample size, the probability of this distribution occurring by chance is, in the paper's language, "essentially zero." The researchers applied the methodology used by the FBI and SEC in conventional insider trading cases: pattern analysis, clustering, position size relative to market liquidity, and timing of entry relative to news.

The FBI Raid and the Dropped Investigation

In late October 2024, FBI agents arrived at the New York apartment of Shayne Coplan, CEO of Polymarket. They seized his phone. No public explanation was given for what triggered the investigation or what they were looking for. Federal investigations of this type — targeting the CEO of a financial platform — are not opened without a threshold of established probable cause.

In July 2025, both the DOJ investigation and a parallel CFTC investigation were dropped simultaneously. No charges. No explanation. No press release from the government explaining why an investigation that justified a CEO's phone seizure was closed without action. In the same month, Polymarket paid $112 million to acquire QCEX, a CFTC-licensed exchange, giving Polymarket the domestic license it needed to operate legally in the US.

Five weeks later, Donald Trump Jr. announced an equity investment of "double-digit millions" in Polymarket through his venture fund, 1789 Capital. Trump Jr. simultaneously serves as a paid advisor to Kalshi, Polymarket's direct competitor. He disclosed neither relationship when commenting on prediction markets in media appearances during the 2024 campaign.

06, The Congo Parallel

Same structure. Different suit. Different continent.

The parallel is not rhetorical. It is structural. The organization that identified the nine wallets on Polymarket is the same organization that broke the Congo Hold-Up — the largest financial data leak analysis in African history.

What the Congo Hold-Up was

In 2021, a coalition of 19 international investigative media outlets published the Congo Hold-Up, based on 3.5 million leaked documents from BGFIBank's DRC subsidiary. The investigation revealed how elites in the Democratic Republic of Congo used a bank connected to the president's family to siphon public funds, launder money through shell companies, and extract billions from a country where 60% of the population lives in extreme poverty. PPLAAF (the Platform to Protect Whistleblowers in Africa) and Mediapart (France) led the investigation. ACDC co-authored it.

The structural mechanism: a small number of connected insiders used a financial intermediary, regulatory capture of domestic oversight institutions, and cross-border obfuscation to extract public value for private gain. The victims were the people of the DRC.

ACDC's role in both

The Alliance for Countering Crime Online does quantitative financial crime analysis. Their methodology in the Congo Hold-Up was to apply pattern analysis to the leaked BGFIBank documents — following transaction clusters, timing, and counterparties to identify the mechanism of extraction. They applied an identical methodology to Polymarket: reviewing all 435,672 markets, clustering wallets by behavioral patterns, and measuring win rates against statistical baselines.

The Congo Hold-Up took years and 19 newsrooms. The Polymarket analysis was published by two researchers, submitted to the CFTC, and has received no government response. This film exists in the space between the evidence and the reckoning.
