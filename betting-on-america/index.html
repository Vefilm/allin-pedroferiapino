The Legal Architecture

It's not insider trading.
Not technically.

That is the entire problem. The crime that prediction markets enable has no proper name. The laws that would stop it don't exist yet. And the people who could write them invested in the platforms first.

The Crime Has No Name

Prediction market contracts are classified as event contracts under CFTC jurisdiction — they are futures and commodities, not securities. The SEC has no authority. "Insider trading" is an SEC concept. When Christopher Van Dyke placed 13 bets using information he wasn't supposed to have, the DOJ could not charge him with insider trading. They charged him with wire fraud and commodities fraud — improvised legal theories applied to a crime the law doesn't yet recognize.

How the CFTC Is Supposed to Work

The Commodity Futures Trading Commission is governed by five commissioners confirmed by the Senate. No more than three can be from the same political party. This bipartisan structure is not courtesy — it is a statutory safeguard written into the Commodity Exchange Act. It exists specifically to prevent any single political agenda from controlling markets regulation.

Commissioners vote on enforcement decisions, rulemaking, and major policy changes. Dissents are public record. The minority can force explanations. The structure creates accountability through disagreement.

The Critical Distinction

Regulatory capture and regulatory deployment are not the same thing. One is a corruption of the system. The other is the system working as designed — just for the wrong people.

Regulatory Capture — The Traditional Problem

Regulatory capture is when an agency meant to regulate an industry becomes so influenced by that industry that it stops regulating effectively. It happens through the revolving door — regulators become lobbyists, industry executives become commissioners. Over time, the agency adopts the industry's worldview, stops enforcement, becomes passive.

Classic capture is a corruption story. The system was supposed to work one way; it was gradually subverted. It produces regulatory paralysis — an agency that stops doing its job.

Example: The FDA slowly becoming more deferential to pharmaceutical industry timelines. SEC slowing enforcement against major banks. The regulator becomes slow, toothless, captured.
The danger level: High. But reversible. New leadership, new priorities, new enforcement posture. The institution survives.
Regulatory Deployment — What We Are Seeing Now

Regulatory deployment is different in kind, not just degree. The regulator is not passive — it is actively weaponized. It doesn't just stop enforcing against an industry; it goes to court to protect that industry from other regulators. It doesn't just defer to executives — it installs them on advisory councils that replace independent experts.

Deployment leaves artifacts. Court precedents that protect the platforms. Regulatory frameworks written by the platforms' executives. Policy positions that survive the commissioner who created them.

What we're documenting: CFTC files 50 court briefs defending Polymarket and Kalshi from state oversight. Sues states that tried to restrict prediction markets. Enforcement staff drops 80%. CEO council replaces expert advisory body. This is not a passive agency. This is an active deployment.
The danger level: Critical. Structural. The next CFTC chair inherits 50 court precedents defending the platforms, a disbanded advisory committee, and an enforcement culture that will take years to rebuild. The damage outlasts the appointment.
Capture asks: how did the regulator stop working?
Deployment asks: who is the regulator working for now?

The DARPA precedent is the proof. In 2003, the regulator (Congress) worked correctly — it identified a structural danger and shut it down. In 2026, the regulator (CFTC) has been deployed to ensure the identical danger cannot be shut down, no matter what the evidence shows.

The Precedent · 2003

Congress saw this clearly in 2003. The argument was simple: if you can profit from an assassination, you now have a financial reason to cause one.

DARPA · Policy Analysis Market · 2002–2003

DARPA proposed allowing intelligence analysts to bet on geopolitical events — terrorist attacks, assassinations, coups, economic shocks. The theory was sound: markets aggregate dispersed information better than committees. Analysts who privately believed an attack was coming could signal that belief through a bet, even if they couldn't say it directly.

Congress called it "morally repugnant" and "unbelievably stupid." Senator Ron Wyden: "This appears to encourage terrorism and is an incentive to murder." Shut down in 72 hours. Lead architect Admiral John Poindexter resigned.

The objection was not about information quality. The objection was about incentive structure. If you can profit from a geopolitical outcome, you now have a financial reason to cause it — especially if you have the power to cause it.
Polymarket / Kalshi · CFTC-Approved · 2023–2026

The identical product — betting on geopolitical and military events — is now CFTC-approved, processing $54.4 billion in wagers across 435,672 markets. It includes contracts on military strikes, ceasefire announcements, oil price movements following presidential statements, and the outcomes of specific military operations.

The danger Congress identified in 2003 is documented in 2026: Army Sergeant Van Dyke placed 13 bets using classified knowledge of a military operation. A nine-wallet network won 98% of its bets on Iran military outcomes. $1.75 billion in oil futures moved hours before presidential announcements.

The CFTC that approved this has not investigated any of the above. It is run by one commissioner. His former client is building a competing platform with the president's company. His ethics recusal expires mid-2026.
