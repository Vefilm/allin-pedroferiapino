Chapter 01

Tom’s Eyes


The moment Tom places a bet, his pupils dilate. This is not voluntary. This is the sympathetic nervous system preparing Tom for a threat or an opportunity. His brain cannot tell the difference.

The visual cortex prioritizes the screen. Peripheral vision narrows. Tom’s field of awareness shrinks to the size of a phone. This is called attentional tunneling. It is the same thing that happens to a fighter pilot in a dogfight.

Tom is not in a dogfight. Tom is on his couch.
The Design

App interfaces are built to exploit this tunnel. Red and green color coding, flashing odds, countdown timers. Every element is engineered to hold the narrowed gaze. The app sees Tom’s attention. The app is keeping it.

Chapter 02

Tom’s Brain


The nucleus accumbens, the brain’s reward center, releases dopamine in anticipation of the outcome. Not after a win. Before it is decided. The uncertainty is the drug.

This is called variable-ratio reinforcement. It is the most powerful behavioral conditioning schedule known to science. It is the same mechanism that drives slot machines. The FanDuel interface was built by people who studied this. They studied it carefully.

Tom’s brain is not broken. It is working exactly as designed. Someone else designed it.
The Loop

With micro-betting (wagering on individual pitches, plays, and possessions) the dopamine loop fires every 90 seconds. A four-hour game is not four hours. It is 160 individual bets. 160 individual dopamine pulses. Tom’s reward system does not distinguish between a $5 and a $500 bet.

Chapter 03

Tom’s Heart


Tom’s resting heart rate is 68 beats per minute. When he places a live bet during a fourth-quarter comeback, it reaches 112. When the bet is pending, it does not drop. It holds. Tom’s cardiovascular system is responding to a perceived survival event.

Cortisol and adrenaline are released. Blood pressure rises. This is the same physiological state as physical danger. Tom’s body does not know he is watching sports. Tom’s body thinks Tom is being chased.

Tom is not being chased. Tom has been sitting still for six hours.
Research Note

Studies of problem gamblers show cardiovascular indicators consistent with chronic stress disorder: elevated baseline cortisol, disrupted sleep architecture, and suppressed immune response. The body keeps the score. Tom’s body is keeping it.

Chapter 04

Tom’s Bloodstream


Adrenaline (epinephrine) spikes. Glucose floods the bloodstream to fuel the muscles Tom is not using. His liver releases glycogen. His digestive system shuts down. His body is prepared to run or fight. He is scrolling through odds on DraftKings.

After the bet resolves, win or lose, the crash. Cortisol levels fall. Serotonin dips. Tom feels flat. Empty. The obvious solution is to place another bet. Tom’s brain agrees. This is called the chasing reflex. It is not a character flaw. It is chemistry.

The app knew about the chasing reflex. The app counted on it.
Chapter 05

Tom’s Good Sense


The prefrontal cortex is where Tom keeps his judgment. Risk assessment. Impulse control. The part of the brain that says: Tom, you cannot afford this. Tom, you have been at this for four hours. Tom, it is 2 a.m.

Under sustained dopamine flooding and cortisol elevation, prefrontal cortex activity measurably decreases. Tom’s judgment center goes partially offline. This is not metaphor. This is observable in brain imaging. Tom’s good sense has a light switch. The app has found the switch.

Tom is making financial decisions with the lights off.
The VIP Host

This is precisely when FanDuel VIP hosts are trained to call. Not when Tom is rested and rational. When Tom is deep in the session, prefrontal cortex suppressed, chasing reflex active. Terry Thompson is now a named plaintiff in the March 2026 federal lawsuit. He and fellow plaintiff Christopher Sage lost over two million dollars combined. His VIP host messaged him hundreds of times. She knew when to call.

Chapter 06

The Loop


Bet. Spike. Crash. Chase. Bet again. The cycle compresses over time. Sessions grow longer. Stakes grow larger. Tom is no longer betting for fun. Tom is betting to return to baseline. Tom needs the spike to feel normal.

This is the clinical definition of addiction. It applies to gambling with the same neurological precision as opioids. Peer-reviewed research published in April 2026 confirms the clinical scale: people with gambling disorder carry an 8.84 times elevated suicide risk compared to the general population. For Americans aged 20 to 49 — Tom's demographic — that multiplier is 19.3. The National Council on Problem Gambling puts the outcome plainly: 1 in 5 people with a gambling problem will attempt suicide in their lifetime.

Higher than alcohol. Higher than opioids. Higher than any other addiction on record.
