The Story

A Crime So New the Law Hasn't Named It.

A CFTC-approved prediction market. A president's family holding the equity. A regulator who is their former client's business partner. Nine anonymous wallets that bet on American military operations with a 98% win rate and $2.4 million in profit. The $800 million oil short that moved fifteen minutes before the Trump Truth Social post that moved oil prices.

This is not a film about sports gambling. Addiction is the front door, not the building. The building is a captured financial market, dressed up as a game, that moves in real time with information the public doesn't have.

Visual Approach

The Cinematography Is the Argument.

"The DP chooses what the audience gets to see. It's not a technical job, it's a moral one."

Three visual registers, each earned by what it's describing. Verite for the investigators at work: Deebs at the screens, Kovel on the phone, Hornsby in Paris. The Citizenfour register. Unhurried. On the record.

Stylized for the machine itself: the hangar, the empty CFTC commission table, the bet entering the dark. The mechanism, made visible. The gap between what the public can see and what is actually happening, rendered as image.

Archive for the record that already exists: 2003 DARPA hearings, congressional testimony, court documents. The public record, cut cinematically to show that none of this is new.

Touchstones: Citizenfour's intimacy with the investigator. Crime of the Century's institutional clarity. All the Beauty and the Bloodshed's structural patience.
